===========================================================
THE BIG IDEA (explain it like I'm 5)
===========================================================

Imagine you're learning to spike a volleyball.
Normally you'd need a real coach standing next to you saying:
"Hey, your arm isn't pulling back far enough before you hit the ball."

We built a computer program that does exactly that — automatically.

You upload a video of yourself playing volleyball.
The computer watches it, figures out what you're doing wrong,
and tells you how to fix it — with words, with a drill, and with a video
showing you side-by-side with an Olympic-level player.

No human coach needed. Just upload a video and get feedback.

That's the whole thing. Everything else below is just HOW it does that.

THE TOOLS WE DOWNLOADED (not code we wrote — tools we plugged in)
===========================================================

1. YOLO11x-pose  (file: data_collection/yolo11x-pose.pt)
   -------------------------------------------------------
   Think of this like a pair of magic glasses.
   You show it a video frame and it draws 17 dots on every person it sees.
   One dot on each shoulder, elbow, wrist, hip, knee, ankle, and the face.
   Like a stick figure but very precise.
   It's right about 70%+ of the time per joint.
   Made by a company called Ultralytics. Free to use.

2. MotionAGFormer  (inside models/AthletePose3D/)
   -----------------------------------------------
   YOLO gives us flat 2D dots (like a drawing on paper).
   MotionAGFormer takes those flat dots and figures out the DEPTH —
   so it knows not just WHERE your elbow is on screen,
   but HOW FAR it is from the camera.
   Like going from a flat drawing to a 3D sculpture.
   It was trained specifically on athletes (12 sports).
   It's 69% more accurate than generic models for sports movements.

3. VolleyVision  (inside models/VolleyVision/)
   ---------------------------------------------
   A tool trained on 14,000 volleyball images.
   It watches a video and says "that's a spike" or "that's a block."
   It knows 5 actions: block, defense, serve, set, spike.
   It's right 92% of the time. Very good.
   Made by someone on GitHub (shukkkur). Free to use.

4. Groq + Llama 3.3 70B  (used inside coach_feedback.py)
   --------------------------------------------------------
   This is the AI that writes the coaching advice in plain English.
   Groq is a company that runs AI models very fast and for free.
   Llama 3.3 is the actual AI brain — made by Meta (Facebook's parent company).
   It's free, gives 14,400 requests per day.
   This is the only part that needs the internet to work.
   (We tried Google Gemini first but hit quota limits, then switched to Groq.)

5. ByteTrack  (used inside player_tracker.py)
   --------------------------------------------
   Follows multiple people in a video at the same time.
   Can track up to 12 players (6 vs 6 team video).
   Gives each player a number and keeps that number on them
   even when they move around or briefly go off screen.

HOW IT ALL WORKS — step by step
===========================================================

When you upload a video, here's what happens in order:

STEP 1 — VIDEO QUALITY CHECK  (video_quality.py)
   Before doing anything expensive, the computer checks:
   - Is the video too blurry? (shaky camera = bad)
   - Is it too dark or too bright?
   - Is the resolution at least 480p?
   - Is the video between 0.5 seconds and 5 minutes long?
   - Is a person actually visible in the frame?
   If any of these fail, it stops and tells you exactly what's wrong
   and how to fix it. No wasted time on bad footage.

STEP 2 — ACTION DETECTION  (action_detector.py)
   VolleyVision scans the video and finds every moment where
   a volleyball action happens (spike, serve, block, etc.)
   It uses a sliding window of 5 frames — needs to see the same
   action 3 times in a row before it counts it as real.
   This stops it from getting confused by random movements.

STEP 3 — CLIP EXTRACTION  (action_localiser.py)
   Once it knows WHERE the spike (or serve, or block) happens,
   it cuts out just that clip — a few seconds around the action.
   This means the heavy analysis only runs on the relevant part,
   not the whole video.

STEP 4 — POSE EXTRACTION  (pose_extractor.py)
   YOLO watches every frame of the clip and draws 17 dots on the player.
   Output: a list of dot positions for every frame.
   If the video is 60 frames, you get 60 sets of 17 dots.
   This is called a "pose sequence."

STEP 5 — 3D LIFTING  (pose_3d_lifter.py)
   MotionAGFormer takes those flat 2D dots and adds depth.
   It looks at 81 frames at a time (about 2.7 seconds at 30fps)
   and figures out the 3D position of every joint.
   Output: (frames × 17 joints × 3 coordinates) — a 3D skeleton over time.

STEP 6 — BIOMECHANICS MEASUREMENT  (kinematics.py + pose_extractor.py)
   Now we measure the actual body mechanics:
   - Joint angles (how bent is your elbow? your knee?)
   - Joint velocities (how fast is your arm moving?)
   - A Butterworth filter smooths out the wobbly measurements
     (like noise-cancelling headphones but for numbers)
   
   For a SPIKE specifically, it measures:
   - arm_cock_angle: how far back your hitting arm pulls (like drawing a bow)
   - jump_height: how high you get off the ground
   - approach_speed: how fast your 3-step run-up is
   - contact_point: where your hand meets the ball relative to your body
   - follow_through: how fully your arm swings after hitting

STEP 7 — PHASE DETECTION  (phase_detector.py)
   The movement is split into phases:
   Spike:  APPROACH → TAKEOFF → ARM_COCK → CONTACT → FOLLOW_THROUGH
   Serve:  STANCE → TOSS → ARM_COCK → CONTACT → FOLLOW_THROUGH
   Block:  READY → STEP → JUMP → REACH → LAND
   Dig:    READY → MOVE → LOW_POSITION → CONTACT → RECOVERY
   
   This is important because we compare your CONTACT phase
   to the elite's CONTACT phase — not just frame 1 to frame 1.
   That's called phase-matching and it makes the comparison fair.

STEP 8 — ELITE COMPARISON  (analyser.py + reference_library.py)
   We have 47 processed videos of volleyball players at different levels.
   The reference library loads all the elite ones and builds an average.
   It normalises everything to 100 frames so a 400-frame video
   and a 100-frame video can be compared fairly.
   
   Your measurements get compared to the elite average.
   Each metric gets a verdict: GOOD or NEEDS IMPROVEMENT.
   Overall verdict: ELITE (all good) / GOOD (60%+ good) / NEEDS WORK.

STEP 9 — AI COACHING FEEDBACK  (coach_feedback.py)
   The numbers go to the Groq AI (Llama 3.3).
   For every problem found, the AI writes:
   - What is actually happening in your body (1 sentence, plain English)
   - What it should FEEL like when done correctly (sensation words)
   - A specific drill with exact sets and reps
   - Why that drill works
   
   The language adapts to your level:
   - Beginner: "pull your elbow back like drawing a bow"
   - Intermediate: "load your hips on the penultimate step"
   - Advanced: "increase shoulder abduction angle at arm cocking phase"
   
   If the same problem shows up 3 sessions in a row,
   the drill prescription escalates to something harder.

STEP 10 — VIDEO RENDERING  (skeleton_overlay.py)
   Two videos get made:
   
   Video 1 — Annotated Video:
   Your original video with a skeleton drawn on you.
   Green joints = doing well. Red joints = needs work.
   A faint grey ghost skeleton shows where an elite athlete would be
   at that exact same moment in the movement.
   A panel on the right shows all your scores.
   
   Video 2 — Side-by-Side Comparison:
   Left: your video with your skeleton.
   Right: the elite skeleton animated next to yours.
   Orange arrows show where YOUR joints are vs where they SHOULD be.
   A banner shows which phase you're in.

STEP 11 — PROGRESS TRACKING  (progress_tracker.py)
   Every session gets saved to a file for that athlete.
   Over time it tracks:
   - Is each metric improving, declining, or stuck (plateau)?
   - Are there injury risk patterns?
     (e.g. landing_balance below 0.6 = unstable landing = knee injury risk)
   - What's your best session ever?
   
   Injury risk flags trigger BEFORE you get hurt, not after.


===========================================================
THE API — the door to the whole system
===========================================================

api.py is the file that runs the web server.
Think of it like a restaurant:
- Your app or website = the customer
- The API = the waiter
- Our system = the kitchen

The customer says "I want the side-by-side comparison for this spike video."
The waiter (API) takes the video to the kitchen (our code).
The kitchen analyses it and gives back the annotated video.
The waiter delivers it back.

HOW TO START IT:
   Open a terminal and run:
   cd C:\sportsai-backend
   uvicorn api:app --reload --host 0.0.0.0 --port 8000

   Then open your browser and go to:
   http://localhost:8000/docs

   You'll see a page with every available option.
   Click any one, click "Try it out", upload a video, click Execute.

THE FULL MENU (all 15+ things it can do):
   POST /analyse/auto          → Full auto-detection, no hints needed (MAIN ONE TO USE)
   POST /analyse/video         → Returns annotated MP4 with skeleton overlay
   POST /analyse/compare       → Returns side-by-side vs elite MP4
   POST /analyse/spike         → Biomechanics numbers for a spike
   POST /analyse/serve         → Biomechanics numbers for a serve
   POST /analyse/block         → Biomechanics numbers for a block
   POST /analyse/dig           → Biomechanics numbers for a dig
   POST /analyse/action        → What action is happening in the video?
   POST /analyse/court         → Where is the court in the video?
   POST /analyse/players       → Track all players with IDs
   POST /analyse/ball          → Track the ball and detect rallies
   POST /analyse/full          → Run EVERYTHING in one go
   POST /check-video           → Check if video is good enough before analysing
   POST /visualise/heatmap     → Court heatmap (where did players spend their time?)
   POST /visualise/pose-chart  → Joint angle graph over time
   POST /visualise/tracking    → Tracking video with all players labelled
   POST /visualise/full        → All visualisations at once
   GET  /progress/{id}/{tech}  → See how an athlete has improved over time


===========================================================
THE TRAINING DATA — what we collected
===========================================================

We downloaded 51 YouTube volleyball videos using batch_processor.py.
The list of URLs is in: data/input/volleyball_urls.csv

Of those 51 videos, 47 have been fully processed (for SPIKE only):
- Elite level: Olympic/professional players
- Advanced level: high-level club players
- Intermediate level: college/recreational players

For each processed video, two files get saved:
- vb_spike_advanced_18_pose3d.npy     ← the 3D skeleton data (numbers)
- vb_spike_advanced_18_biomechanics.json  ← the measurements (angles, speeds)

The metadata.json file keeps a log of everything that's been processed.

WHAT'S MISSING:
- Serve data: URLs exist in the CSV but haven't been processed yet
- Block data: same
- Dig data: same
So right now the system works best for SPIKES. The other techniques
have the code ready but no training data to compare against yet.


===========================================================
THE CONFIG FILE — volleyball_techniques.json
===========================================================

This file (config/volleyball_techniques.json) defines what to measure
for each of the 4 techniques. Think of it as the rulebook.

For a spike it says: measure arm_cock_angle, jump_height, approach_speed,
contact_point, and follow_through — and what units each one is in.

For a dig it says: measure platform_angle, knee_bend, hip_drop,
arm_extension, and recovery_position.

This file is what you'd edit if you wanted to add a new metric
or change what gets measured.

===========================================================
QUICK SUMMARY — what this project is in one paragraph
===========================================================

We built an AI volleyball coach that runs on your laptop.
You upload a video of someone playing volleyball.
The computer draws a skeleton on them, measures how their body moves,
compares it to Olympic-level athletes, and tells them exactly what to fix —
with plain-English advice, a specific drill, and a side-by-side video
showing them next to the correct movement.
It works for spikes right now (47 training videos collected).
Serve, block, and dig code is ready but needs training data collected.
The whole thing runs through a web API at http://localhost:8000/docs.
The only thing that needs the internet is the AI coaching text (Groq/Llama).
