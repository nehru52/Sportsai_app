Every layer builds on the previous. Layers 1–2 are existing tools. Layers 3–5 are your original work — and your moat.

Two-person simultaneous pose extraction

YOLO11x-Pose in multi-person mode. ByteTrack assigns Fighter A (red skeleton) and Fighter B (blue skeleton) persistent IDs from frame 1. Physics constraint: two human bodies cannot occupy the same skeleton space — prevents limb misattribution during clinch. Frames with confidence below 0.65 are flagged and excluded from scoring — never feed garbage frames to the analysis engine.

Core technical challenge

TWO-PERSON TRACKING IN COMBAT

This is the hardest technical problem in sports AI. Here is the honest approach — including what fails and how to handle it gracefully.

The fundamental problem: When two wrestlers clinch or an MMA fighter is in guard position, standard pose models assign one fighter's arm to the other fighter's body. The CVPR 2025 research uses 8+ cameras and physics simulation to solve this. You have 1 phone camera. Here is how you build around that constraint instead of pretending it doesn't exist.

The 3-tier confidence system

CLEAN
Both fighters separated, conf >0.75

Full technique scoring active. Both fighters' joint angles recorded with high accuracy. Rules engine runs at full fidelity. These frames count toward technique scores and dossier patterns.

CONTACT
Clinch or grappling range, conf 0.5–0.75

Technique scoring paused. Interaction events still recorded (clinch entry, break timing, control position). Partial data used for grappling pattern detection only. Display: "Contact phase" indicator in the UI.

LOST
Ground work or occlusion, conf <0.5

Frames discarded entirely. Temporal interpolation fills the visual gap smoothly. Never show athletes data from lost frames. Ground work analysis is a Phase 3 feature requiring multi-camera setup.

# Two-person combat tracking with confidence-based tier system
# This is the most important 100 lines in the entire codebase

from ultralytics import YOLO
import numpy as np

model = YOLO('yolo11x-pose.pt')  # fine-tuned checkpoint

def process_combat_frame(frame, prev_assignments):
    results = model.track(frame, persist=True, tracker="bytetrack.yaml")
    
    fighters = {}
    for i, box in enumerate(results[0].boxes):
        track_id = int(box.id)
        kpts = results[0].keypoints.xyn[i].numpy()
        conf = results[0].keypoints.conf[i].numpy()
        
        # Assign consistent Fighter A/B IDs across frames
        fighter_id = resolve_fighter_identity(track_id, prev_assignments)
        
        # Calculate mean confidence across load-bearing joints only
        # (hips, knees, shoulders — not fingers)
        key_joints = [5,6,11,12,13,14,15,16]
        mean_conf = conf[key_joints].mean()
        
        fighters[fighter_id] = {
            "keypoints": kpts,
            "confidence": mean_conf,
            "tier": get_confidence_tier(mean_conf),
            "biomechanics": extract_biomechanics(kpts) if mean_conf > 0.65 else None
        }
    
    # Detect interaction state
    interaction = detect_interaction(fighters)
    
    return {
        "fighters": fighters,
        "interaction": interaction,   # 'separated', 'clinch', 'ground'
        "scoreable": interaction == "separated" and
                      all(f["confidence"] > 0.65 for f in fighters.values())
    }

def detect_interaction(fighters):
    if len(fighters) < 2: return "single"
    
    a_hip = fighters["A"]["keypoints"][11]  # left hip
    b_hip = fighters["B"]["keypoints"][11]
    distance = np.linalg.norm(a_hip - b_hip)
    
    a_hip_height = fighters["A"]["keypoints"][11][1]
    b_hip_height = fighters["B"]["keypoints"][11][1]
    
    if distance < 0.25: return "clinch"
    if a_hip_height > 0.75 or b_hip_height > 0.75: return "ground"
    return "separated"

The feedback interrupt rules — critical design decision

You cannot give feedback every 500ms or you destroy the fight. The system must decide: is this observation important enough to interrupt the athlete or coach right now? There are only 3 levels of interrupt, and only Level 1 fires during the live fight. Levels 2 and 3 are between rounds and post-fight.
LIVE

Level 1 — Immediate safety / high-value pattern (fires during fight, vibration alert)

Only fires when: (a) injury risk indicator detected (knee valgus, spine hyperextension), or (b) opponent has shown the same exploitable pattern 3+ times in the last 90 seconds. Maximum 1 Level 1 alert per minute. Goes to coach's tablet as a vibration + one-line text.

⚡ PATTERN: Opp drops right guard 0.3s after body shot — seen 3× in last 80s. Counter window open.
ROUND

Level 2 — Between rounds (60-second corner report)

Delivered to coach's tablet when the round ends. Contains: this round's technique score vs previous round, top 2 errors detected, opponent's most common pattern this round, and 1 tactical adjustment. Presented as bullet points — coach reads it in 15 seconds while treating the fighter.

Round 2 summary: Your level change ↓ (entry avg 0.74, was 0.68). Opp shot 71% to your left — circle right. Guard dropped after own combos ×4. Fix: extend your 1-2 by one beat before resetting.
POST

Level 3 — Full post-fight breakdown (complete analysis)

Available within 60 seconds of the final bell. Contains: complete timeline with all events annotated, technique scores per round with trend, error frequency by phase, opponent pattern map, head-to-head comparison, and the full personalized improvement plan. Each fighter receives their private version. The shared breakdown unlocks if both consent.

Fight breakdown: 12 technique events scored. Your entry improved R1→R3 (+8pts). Critical gap: finish phase (avg 42/100). Opp exploited this in 4 of 7 successful counters. Primary drill: penetration step finishing drill ×50/day.
Corner intelligence

THE CORNER AI TABLET

The coach's interface during a live fight. Designed to be read in 2 seconds, not studied. Every decision in a corner has to be instant.

Corner tablet screen design — exactly 4 elements

🔴

Live score bars

Two horizontal bars — one per fighter — updating in real time. Color-coded: green above 70, amber 50–70, red below 50. Coach sees at a glance who is dominating technically.

⚡

Pattern alert feed

Single scrolling line of the most recent patterns detected. Maximum 3 lines visible at once. The most recent pattern is always at top. Disappears after 2 minutes if not repeated.

📊

Round comparison

Mini chart showing this round vs last round per fighter. One number: "Your fighter: +6 pts this round." Tells the coach if the tactical adjustment from last corner is working.

🎯

The one thing

A single sentence at the bottom: the single most important thing the coach should tell their fighter this round. Generated by Claude in under 200 tokens. This is the product's highest-value output per word.

"The one thing" prompt design matters enormously. The system prompt: "You are a combat sports corner coach. You have 30 seconds to give one instruction. Based on the data, what is the single most important adjustment your fighter must make? Maximum 15 words. Be specific and actionable. Never generic." That constraint produces: "Circle right after your jab — his left cross is slower than his right" not "keep your guard up."
Output design

THREE REPORTS, THREE PURPOSES

Every 1v1 session produces exactly three outputs. Each serves a different purpose. Each has a different audience. Neither fighter sees the other's private report.

🔒 Fighter A Private Report

Visible only to Fighter A. Contains: technique score vs personal history, phase-by-phase breakdown, specific errors with video timestamps (-2s before each error), elite benchmark comparison, and a 2-drill training prescription targeting the weakest phase. Fighter B never sees this report.

🔒 Fighter B Private Report

Same structure as Fighter A's. Completely independent. Fighter B's errors are framed in terms of their own progression history — not compared to Fighter A. Training prescriptions based on their specific weak phases. Fighter A never sees this report.

🤝 Shared Tactical Breakdown (requires mutual consent — both tap "Share")

The shareable version. Contains: fight timeline with key events annotated, who controlled range and when it shifted, the decisive technique difference, and the head-to-head rivalry stats update. This is what athletes post on TikTok and Instagram. The shareable breakdown has Fighter A's avatar (red) and Fighter B's avatar (blue) animated through the fight's key moments with skeleton overlays. It is a 30-second shareable video clip. This is your organic growth engine.

Olympic grade specifications

OLYMPIC-GRADE TECHNICAL STANDARDS

What Olympic programs use, what you match, and what you realistically cannot match with a phone — plus why it doesn't matter for your market.

📷

Camera setup

2–4

Olympic: 8–16 cameras, 4K, 240fps. Yours: 2 phones (consumer Phase 2+), 1080p, 60fps. The technique quality delta between 2-camera and 8-camera is real — but 2 cameras eliminates 80% of the occlusion problem. Your product guide must include a camera placement diagram for Phase 3 Corner Mode.

⚡

Analysis latency

<500ms

Olympic systems: sub-100ms with dedicated server rooms at venue. Yours: <500ms via WebSocket over gym WiFi. For corner coaching, this is completely acceptable — the coach absorbs the data over 5–10 seconds between rounds anyway.

🎯

Accuracy target

±8°

Olympic motion capture: ±2mm (sub-millimetric). Your YOLO11 fine-tuned: ±8° joint angle error. Research shows ±8° is clinically meaningful for technique coaching. Not laboratory biomechanics — but the difference between "coach can trust it" and "coach ignores it."

🏅

Validation standard

75%

Your target: 75% AI/coach agreement rate before calling it Olympic-grade. Below 65%: coaches override constantly, product feels broken. 65–75%: usable, improving. Above 75%: coaches trust the AI, it accelerates their work rather than adding friction. This is your ship/don't-ship criterion.

📊

Metrics per session

47+

Olympic sports science programs track 40–60 biomechanical parameters per session. Your system extracts: 33 keypoints × 3 values (x,y,conf) per frame + 14 derived joint angles + 6 velocity vectors + interaction event count = 47+ metrics per session, every session.

🔒

Privacy standard

GDPR+

Olympic biometric data is protected by athlete consent agreements and national body policies. Yours: GDPR compliant (EU), COPPA for under-18, explicit dual-consent for any 1v1 session, skeleton-only storage (no raw video of non-consenting individuals), and right-to-deletion for all biometric data.

Proving it works

VALIDATION PROTOCOL

How you prove the system is Olympic-grade before you call it that. Three validation stages, each with a pass/fail threshold.

Stage 1 — Pose accuracy validation (before launch)

Film 50 sparring sessions with known ground truth (have a biomechanist manually measure joint angles on a subset of frames). Compare YOLO11 outputs to ground truth. Target: mean absolute error below 8° for hip, knee, and shoulder angles. If above 12°: re-fine-tune on more data before shipping. This is the objective measurement that makes "Olympic grade" a defensible claim, not marketing language.

Stage 2 — Coach agreement rate (first 3 months post-launch)

Every time a human coach reviews a 1v1 session and sees the AI's error report, they agree/disagree/edit. Track the agreement rate. Target: 75%+ by month 3. Below 65% at month 2: convene an emergency coach knowledge session to recalibrate thresholds. The divergence data tells you exactly which rules are wrong.

Stage 3 — Outcome correlation (6 months post-launch)

The ultimate validation: do athletes who follow the AI's training prescriptions actually improve their competition results? Survey your most active users at 6 months. Correlate: sessions analyzed vs. tournament win rate vs. technique score progression. If AI-coached athletes win more often than non-AI-coached athletes in the same skill bracket — that is your Olympic-grade proof point. That is your press release. That is your Series A data slide.

Physical setup

HARDWARE SETUP GUIDE

Phase 1 — Drill Mode (one phone, one angle)

Equipment: one phone, one tripod or stable surface. Camera placement: 45° side angle, 3–4 meters from the athletes, hip-height. Records the full session. Upload after. This is enough for technique scoring in striking sports. Grappling loses accuracy during clinch — acknowledged limitation.

Phase 3 — Corner Mode (two phones, WiFi)

Equipment: two iPhones (12 or later), two tripods, gym WiFi minimum 50Mbps. Phone A: front angle (3m from mat). Phone B: rear angle (3m from mat, opposite side). Both run SportsAI camera app simultaneously. System syncs the streams, processes in real-time, and pushes corner alerts. Setup time: 8 minutes with the in-app placement guide.

The placement guide is a product feature, not documentation. Build an AR placement guide directly in the app — point the phone at the mat and the app shows you exactly where to position the tripod with an overlay indicator. "Move left 0.5m — you're in the green zone." This removes the single biggest friction point for first-time Corner Mode setup.
